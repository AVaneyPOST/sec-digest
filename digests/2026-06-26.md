## Telecom

- **Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager — CVE-2026-20245 zero-day confirmed used against a comms service provider (Mandiant report, 24 Jun 2026, HIGH/CRITICAL)**
  Mandiant published its post-mortem of the early-2026 SD-WAN compromise that was previewed in the 4 Jun Cisco PSIRT advisory. The victim is a communications service provider. Attack chain, in order: (1) rogue inter-tenant SD-WAN peering connections appear late 2025; possibly enabled by the earlier auth-bypass zero-days CVE-2026-20127 / CVE-2026-20182. (2) From March 2026, attacker authenticates to vManage using `vmanage-admin`. (3) Uploads a malicious CSV named `evil_tenant.csv` via the tenant-upload CLI feature; CVE-2026-20245 (CVSS 7.8) lets the CSV escape input validation and run as root. (4) Backs up and replaces `/etc/passwd` / `/etc/shadow`. (5) Creates `troot` account with root privilege. (6) Selectively deletes and restores configuration files to cover tracks. Cisco disclosed CVE-2026-20245 on 4 Jun without a patch; fixes started rolling 10 Jun. Patched releases: 20.9.9.2, 20.12.7.2, 20.15.4.5, 20.15.5.3, 20.18.3.1, 26.1.1.2. Telco angle: SD-WAN Manager is the management-plane crown jewel for fleet-managed branch / MEC / 5G transport networks; once an attacker has it they can push arbitrary configuration to edge devices. Audit for `troot` or any unexpected admin account, rogue tenant peerings, and `evil_tenant.csv` artefacts; patch and rotate vManage credentials, not just upgrade. [Mandiant via BleepingComputer: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/mandiant-reveals-how-cisco-sd-wan-zero-day-attacks-gained-root-access/] [CyberScoop: https://cyberscoop.com/cisco-sd-wan-zero-day-exploit-communications-provider/] [SecurityWeek: https://www.securityweek.com/cisco-sd-wan-zero-day-exploited-months-before-patching/] [The Hacker News: https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-zero-day-cve-2026.html] [Security Affairs: https://securityaffairs.com/194200/hacking/cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-zero-day-cve-2026-20245-exploited-months-before-disclosure.html]

- **KDDI managed-email breach — up to 14.22M credentials, six Japanese ISPs affected** *(disclosed 23 Jun; international coverage 24 Jun, HIGH)*
  KDDI, Japan's #2 mobile operator, runs the email-hosting backend for at least six ISPs: STNet, JCOM (J:COM), Chubu Telecommunications, Nifty, Biglobe, and KDDI Web Communications. An attacker exploited a vulnerability in third-party software inside that email stack, KDDI says, getting access to mailbox account data for as many as 14.22M users — including former / dormant customers. Intrusion detected 17 Jun, contained the same day per KDDI's statement; passwords are stored hashed (algorithm unspecified). Direct account takeover not the primary risk: phishing, credential-stuffing, and account-recovery abuse against telco/ISP-tied identities are. Practitioner angle: a managed-email tenant compromise hitting six MNOs/ISPs at once is exactly the kind of multi-tenant supply-chain ISP risk that GSMA / national regulators have been warning about, and credential reuse against XCAP / RCS / customer-portal endpoints should be assumed. [The Register: https://www.theregister.com/cyber-crime/2026/06/24/you-have-got-to-be-kddi-ng-japanese-telco-exposes-142-million-managed-email-credentials/5260555] [Japan Times: https://www.japantimes.co.jp/business/2026/06/24/companies/kddi-data-breach-cyberattack/] [Infosecurity Magazine: https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/kddi-breach-japanese-telcos/] [TechNadu: https://www.technadu.com/kddi-data-breach-exposes-14-2-million-managed-email-credentials/629846/]

## General high-severity

- **Operation Endgame round 2 — Amadey + StealC + SocGholish disrupted; Microsoft files RICO suit** *(24 Jun 2026, HIGH)*
  Europol-coordinated action with Germany's BKA, the Dutch police, Canada, Denmark, the UK and the US. Outcomes: 326 servers seized; 142 domains seized; ~27M stolen credentials recovered; €41M (~$46.5M) in cryptocurrency frozen. SocGholish takedown attributed by Dutch police; SocGholish was the primary distribution path for Evil Corp and a wide downstream of ransomware affiliates. Separately, Microsoft's Digital Crimes Unit filed a US racketeering (RICO) suit treating Amadey and StealC — built by separate developers but sharing infrastructure — as a single criminal enterprise; that's the first time DCU has used that framing. AI tooling (Copilot) was used to do the cross-family infrastructure correlation. In the first two weeks of May 2026 the two families together had ~140k victims. Effect on practitioners: expect short-term silence from Amadey / StealC C2, watch for cut-over to backup infra, and use this as a window to pull credential-stuffing detections forward. [Europol: https://www.europol.europa.eu/media-press/newsroom/news/global-cyber-strike-disrupts-socgholish-amadey-and-stealc-malware-networks] [The Register on Microsoft RICO filing: https://www.theregister.com/security/2026/06/24/microsoft-uses-ai-to-link-two-malware-operations-in-racketeering-suit/5261656] [Help Net Security: https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/06/24/operation-endgame-stealc-amadey-malware-disrupted/] [The Hacker News: https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/amadey-and-stealc-malware-network.html]

- **Mistic backdoor (Symantec) — stealthy, in-memory, self-deleting; tied to access broker KongTuke/Woodgnat** *(disclosed 25 Jun 2026, HIGH — pre-ransomware)*
  Symantec disclosed a new backdoor named Mistic (`Backdoor.Mistic`) used in financially motivated intrusions since April 2026 across insurance, education, IT, and professional-services targets. Loader chain: legitimate signed `MpExtMs.exe` side-loads a malicious `version.dll`, which loads `EndpointDlp.dll` — a name chosen to blend with Microsoft endpoint security tooling. Mistic runs payloads in memory (nothing written to disk for the payload itself) and carries a self-delete kill switch — both hallmarks of long-term low-visibility operator tooling, not commodity crimeware. Attribution: KongTuke / Woodgnat, an initial access broker active since at least 2024, selling footholds to Qilin, Interlock, Rhysida, Akira, 8Base, and Black Basta. Defender play: look for `MpExtMs.exe` side-loading combinations, the named DLL pair above, and unexpected ClickFix-pattern lure delivery, which Symantec also ties to the same broker via the ModeloRAT campaign. [Symantec blog: https://www.security.com/blog-post/new-mistic-backdoor-modelorat] [BleepingComputer: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/stealthy-mistic-backdoor-linked-to-ransomware-access-broker-kongtuke/] [The Register: https://www.theregister.com/security/2026/06/25/self-destructing-mistic-backdoor-linked-to-access-broker-selling-corporate-footholds-to-ransomware-gangs/5262579] [The Hacker News: https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/new-mistic-backdoor-linked-to-kongtuke.html] [Help Net Security: https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/06/25/mistic-backdoor-woodgnat-attacks/]
